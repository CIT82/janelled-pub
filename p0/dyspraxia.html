Dyspraxia

Developmental Coordination Disorder (DCD)

The symptoms of dyspraxia can vary widely among individuals and may change over time. In young children, signs may include delayed milestones such as sitting up, crawling, and walking. School-age children might have difficulty with handwriting, using utensils, or participating in sports. Adults with dyspraxia may face challenges with tasks like driving, organizing paperwork, and maintaining personal grooming. Despite these difficulties, many people with dyspraxia develop effective coping strategies and can lead successful, fulfilling lives with appropriate support and interventions

The Coordination Conundrum


  • Dyspraxia, also known as Developmental Coordination Disorder (DCD), is a neurological condition that affects motor skill development and coordination. It impacts approximately 5-6% of school-aged children and can persist into adulthood. Individuals with dyspraxia often struggle with tasks requiring fine and gross motor skills, such as writing, tying shoelaces, or catching a ball. The condition is not related to intelligence, but it can affect various aspects of daily life, including personal care, academic performance, and social interactions14
